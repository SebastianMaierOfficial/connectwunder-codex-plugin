# Brand OS interview

Use this guide progressively. Ask one to three questions at a time, respond to the user's answers, and skip anything already known.

## Start the conversation

Briefly establish that the process supports existing brands, inspiration-only input, or a blank start.

Begin with:

1. What should the Brand OS help produce over the next six to twelve months?
2. Which brands, products, offers, or people belong in scope?
3. What can the user share now: URLs, files, screenshots, source designs, or inspiration?

If the user mainly asks how it works, explain the collect → inventory → evaluate → visualize → decide → produce → learn loop before asking for material.

## Collect evidence

Invite any of these without requiring all:

- public websites and landing pages;
- logged-in product UI screenshots;
- ZIPs or repositories containing website concepts;
- logos, app icons, favicons, and wordmarks;
- font names or files;
- color values and existing tokens;
- Canva, Figma, PDF, presentation, social, or email designs;
- photos, videos, thumbnails, title cards, and animations;
- external websites or brands the user admires;
- examples the user strongly dislikes.

For each source, learn:

- whether it is live, a concept, legacy, or a reference;
- what the user likes;
- what feels wrong or inconsistent;
- whether it may be shown in a shared or public Gallery;
- whether original files or only screenshots are available.

Do not infer permission to publish private assets.

## Map the organization

Ask only what is needed to distinguish:

- parent company or legal entity;
- personal or founder brand;
- product brands;
- offers, programs, events, and campaigns;
- endorsed relationships between brands;
- brands that may be phased out;
- audiences that require clearly different expressions.

Test the likely architecture:

> Should these brands share one recognizable design DNA while keeping their own names, logos, and product accents?

Do not force a branded-house or house-of-brands model before seeing evidence.

## Learn preferences through concrete examples

Prefer visual reactions over abstract adjectives. When showing or discussing a source, ask:

- What should definitely remain?
- What feels cheap, generic, crowded, cold, playful, or off-brand?
- Which detail creates the strongest sense of quality?
- Which spacing, alignment, or hierarchy feels wrong?
- Would this still fit in a software UI, a landing page, and a document?

Convert answers into testable statements. For example:

- “I like premium” → identify contrast, type scale, whitespace, material, photography, and restraint.
- “The spacing feels wrong” → inspect edge inset, container width, text-to-heading gap, section rhythm, and component density.
- “These apps look unrelated” → identify shared structural tokens and permissible product accents.

## Understand audiences and outcomes

Learn:

- primary and secondary audiences;
- desired perception and trust signal;
- the main business action for each mode;
- required proof: logos, results, testimonials, product screens, authority, or process;
- localization and language requirements;
- accessibility expectations;
- channels where the system must work.

For conversion pages, ask what truthful proof can be used and how each relationship must be labelled. Never turn a platform, customer, or partner logo into an implied endorsement.

## Capture production constraints

Ask when relevant:

- preferred website technology and hosting;
- design tools and AI tools used in the current workflow;
- whether outputs must be editable by nontechnical staff;
- where source assets are stored;
- who approves brand decisions;
- whether the Gallery should be private, workspace-shared, or public;
- how another AI will receive the document and assets.

Do not assume that a link to a workspace document is accessible to an external AI.

## Greenfield path

If little exists, request:

- three to five reference sites or visual examples;
- three desired qualities and three qualities to avoid;
- the first high-value artifact to design;
- real audience and offer copy;
- any existing photo, logo sketch, signature, or product screen.

Create a clearly labelled hypothesis, not a fictional history. Use the first production project as a pilot and feed its learnings back into the system.

## End the initial interview

Stop asking when you can state:

- scope and brand architecture hypothesis;
- available evidence and meaningful gaps;
- required modes;
- user preferences with examples;
- privacy and Gallery access;
- the first useful deliverable.

Summarize what you heard, distinguish facts from hypotheses, and begin the inventory. Do not wait for every possible asset.
